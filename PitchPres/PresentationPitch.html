Introduction

During the Coursera Data Science course (Developing Data Products) it was built a Shiny application. This application shows a spectrum of weather conditions across the world. Another objective was to give a possibility to predict a future weather conditions. Database was built using WunderGround. It is important to state that the application is made for educational purpose and the data should not be use for commercial purpose. There was taken into account a continent location and a Hemisphere during selecting Cities for Database. From a wide palette of weather indicators there was chosen only 4 of them:

  • Mean Temperature Celsius
  • Mean Humidity
  • Mean Pressure h-Pa
  • Mean Wind Speed Km-h

It should be surprising that there is not indicator for raining. The decision was taken because of high unpredictable of raining and lack of records for this indicator.

Prediction Functionality

The Main functionality of the app is to assess predictions of 4 most important indicators. A prediction is calculated by functions from forecast package. In most cases a significant seasonality gives chance for building valuable prediction.
Example for MeanHumidity Berlin:

Holt = HoltWinters(na.approx(ts(unlist(datas_pred[,'MeanHumidity_Berlin']),frequency = 12,start=c(1996,1),end=c(2016,11))))
autoplot(forecast(Holt,h=12))

Weather Conditions during Year - Plots

Many people are interested in Weather conditions across the world. The main aim is to show the difference in a clear manner. Thus it was used a one frame plot of average values of last 20 records - during the year (week interval). There is produced a static plot for a certain indicator and city.
Example for MeanHumidity Berlin:

data_temporary = tempsg %>% as.data.frame() %>% slice(grep("MeanHumidity_Berlin", tempsg$key)) %>%
  na.omit()
ggplot(tempsg,aes(x=week,y=value,color=key))+ geom_smooth(size=1)+
  geom_smooth(data=data_temporary,aes(x=week,y=value,color=key),size=4)+
  theme(legend.position="top")
